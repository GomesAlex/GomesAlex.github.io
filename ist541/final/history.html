Fountain Pen History

Fountain pens evolved from dip pens, such as quill pens, which required the user to dip the pen into a bottle after writing several words. This was a messy process and lacked portability. The idea of having an ink reservoir in the pen is rumored to have begun with Leonardo da Vinci, but it was not until the 1800's when the fountain pen became a dependable writing instrument.

The pen's design revolves around a metal nib that touches the paper, an ink feed that resides behind the pen, and the body of the pen. What has changed and developed over the years is the ink feed technology and filling mechanism. Lewis Edson Waterman and Parker Pen Company contributed to improving feed technology by improving gravity and capillary action so pens wouldn't accidentally dump ink while writing. Filling systems started as ink in the barrel of the pen or a rubber sac and have evolved in many forms. In modern times, the primary filling systems are cartridge/converter and piston filling, allowing for a consistent, quick ink fill that reduces accidental ink dumps or what is known as "burping."

Fountain pens range from simple pens that cost six dollars; vintage pens collectors search for high-end pens that use special techniques to craft works of art with exquisite materials that can be worth tens of thousands of dollars. Here is a great example below, and it is the pride of my collection. A Namiki Urushi Lacquer Vermilion No. 50:

While it appears to be a big plastic pen, it is made from urushiol, a sap from a Japanese tree similar to poison oak. The toxic sap is treated and crafted into a meticulously and labor-intensive lacquer applied one coat at a time and cured in a special humid controlled cabinet for 24-48 hours. This process is repeated up to 60 times to create the incredibly beautiful and highly prized material.
